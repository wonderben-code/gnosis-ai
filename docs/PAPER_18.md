## Abstract

We present the complete discovery catalogue from the first autonomous structural analysis of pure mathematics. Using Gnosis AI — an autonomous knowledge discovery system implementing Convergent Descent — we surveyed 77 established results across five fields of mathematics (topology and geometry, algebraic geometry, category theory, number theory, and analysis), then systematically compared every pair of fields for structural convergences.

The system found 30 validated cross-domain convergences — structural agreements between independent mathematical results — spanning all 10 possible domain pairs. Of these, 17 are formal convergences (identical mathematical structure appearing independently) and 13 are structural analogies (parallel but not formally identical structure). Each convergence was validated through a five-dimensional Epistemic Assurance audit covering strength, independence, adversarial challenge, reproducibility, and depth consistency. Confidence scores range from 0.33 to 0.55.

We present each discovery in full: what the convergence claims, what it means, what evidence supports it, and what is genuinely novel about it. Thematic analysis reveals four structural principles — local-global determination, relational structure primacy, universal duality, and rigidity-canonicity — that unify the 30 discoveries. These four principles reduce through meta-convergence to a terminal fixed point: *"Reality is constraint."*

The analysis ran autonomously in 18 minutes at a cost of $1.81. The full data, including all convergence files, meta-convergence findings, and domain surveys, is publicly available.

**Keywords:** mathematical structure, cross-domain convergence, topology, algebraic geometry, category theory, number theory, analysis, autonomous discovery, structural realism

## 1. Introduction

Mathematics is often described as a unified subject, but this unity is rarely demonstrated systematically. Connections between fields are typically discovered by individual mathematicians who happen to work at the intersection of two areas. The Langlands programme connects number theory and representation theory. Mirror symmetry connects algebraic geometry and string theory. The Atiyah-Singer index theorem connects analysis and topology. These are celebrated results, but they are products of human insight, dependent on who happened to look and where.

This paper asks: what happens when the search for structural connections is conducted systematically, autonomously, and across all pairs of fields simultaneously?

We used Gnosis AI [1] to survey five fields of pure mathematics, then compare every pair for structural convergences. The system operates without human guidance during discovery. It surveys established results, extracts structural conclusions, compares conclusions across domain pairs, validates proposed convergences through adversarial challenge, and searches for patterns across validated convergences. The entire process — 77 results surveyed, 10 domain pairs compared, 30 convergences validated, 12 meta-convergence findings produced, one fixed point reached — completed in 18 minutes.

The result is a catalogue of 30 structural convergences across mathematics — the first such catalogue produced by autonomous analysis. Each convergence identifies a structural agreement between established results from different fields. Each is validated through five independent quality dimensions. And together, they converge on a single structural principle: that mathematical structure, at its irreducible core, is constraint.

This paper presents every discovery, explains what each means, and traces the path from 30 individual convergences to a single fixed point.

### 1.1 Scope and Limitations

This analysis covers five fields of pure mathematics. It does not claim to be comprehensive — 14 fields of mathematics exist in Gnosis AI's taxonomy, and only five were included in this run. The convergences found are those detected by this particular system using this particular methodology on this particular day. Different selections of fields, different survey prompts, or different model temperatures could produce overlapping but not identical results.

We present what was found, not what exists. The catalogue is a first pass, not a final word.

## 3. The Five Domains

### 3.1 Topology and Geometry

The study of shapes, spaces, and their properties that remain unchanged under continuous deformation. The survey identified results spanning classical topology (Poincaré duality, de Rham's theorem), differential geometry (Chern-Weil theory, Atiyah-Singer index theorem), Morse theory, cobordism theory, and the classification of manifolds.

**Key structural conclusions from survey:** Mathematical structures encode their essential information through local-to-global assembly. Topological invariants provide computable handles on global structure from local data.

### 3.2 Algebraic Geometry

The study of geometric objects defined by polynomial equations, bridging algebra and geometry. The survey identified results spanning scheme theory (Grothendieck), cohomology of coherent sheaves, the Weil conjectures, resolution of singularities, minimal model programme, moduli of curves, étale cohomology, Hodge theory, and mirror symmetry.

**Key structural conclusions from survey:** Geometric objects are fundamentally characterised by their algebraic data, and global properties can be recovered from local algebraic information through systematic cohomological methods.

### 3.3 Category Theory

The study of mathematical structure in terms of objects and morphisms — "the mathematics of mathematics." The survey identified results spanning the Yoneda lemma, adjoint functor theorem, Tannaka-Krein duality, Grothendieck topos theory, monadicity theorem, Kan extension formula, cobordism hypothesis, and coherence theorems for monoidal categories.

**Key structural conclusions from survey:** Mathematical objects are fully determined by their relational structure. Category theory provides a language in which the deep unity of apparently different mathematical structures becomes visible.

### 3.4 Number Theory

The study of integers and their generalisations, including algebraic number fields, L-functions, and arithmetic geometry. The survey identified results spanning the modularity theorem, Langlands reciprocity conjecture, class field theory, Chebotarev density theorem, Birch and Swinnerton-Dyer conjecture, quadratic reciprocity, Monstrous Moonshine, prime number theorem, Riemann hypothesis, fundamental theorem of arithmetic, and Dirichlet's unit theorem.

**Key structural conclusions from survey:** Arithmetic structure exhibits deep, often unexpected symmetries and reciprocities. Local arithmetic data frequently determines global arithmetic properties through analytic and algebraic intermediaries.

### 3.5 Analysis

The study of limits, continuity, differentiability, and integration — the rigorous foundation of calculus. The survey identified results spanning the Riesz representation theorem, uniform boundedness principle, spectral theorem for self-adjoint operators, Hahn-Banach theorem, Cauchy's integral theorem, residue theorem, extreme value theorem, Riemann mapping theorem, and Liouville's theorem.

**Key structural conclusions from survey:** Analytical structures exhibit remarkable self-organising properties: completeness prevents pathological behaviour, compactness guarantees existence of extrema, and local regularity conditions propagate to global regularity.

## 5. Local-Global Determination

The most pervasive structural theme across the 30 convergences: local information — singularities, critical points, obstructions, boundary data — completely determines global properties. Seven convergences support this theme directly, spanning five domain pairs.

### 5.1 Local Singularities Determine Global Topology

**Domains:** Topology and Geometry × Algebraic Geometry
**Type:** Formal | **Confidence:** 0.40

**What it claims:** Local algebraic and analytical singularities completely determine global topological invariants through systematic computational procedures.

**What this means:** When you encounter a singularity — a point where something breaks, diverges, or behaves unusually — that local breakdown contains information about the entire global structure. This is not a metaphor. The Atiyah-Singer index theorem proves that the local analytical properties of a differential operator (how it behaves in small neighbourhoods) exactly equal a global topological invariant (a number that characterises the whole space). Morse theory proves that the critical points of a smooth function (the places where the function's gradient vanishes) completely determine the topology of the manifold. In algebraic geometry, the Grothendieck-Riemann-Roch theorem achieves the same thing: local algebraic data in coordinate rings determines global topological invariants through characteristic classes.

**Supporting evidence:**
- *Atiyah-Singer Index Theorem* (Topology): Local analytical properties of differential operators equal global topological invariants. Proven theorem.
- *Grothendieck-Riemann-Roch Theorem* (Algebraic Geometry): Local algebraic data determines global topological invariants through characteristic classes. Proven theorem.
- *Morse Theory* (Topology): Critical points of smooth functions completely determine global topological structure. Proven theorem.

**EA scores:** Strength 0.29 | Independence 0.30 | Adversarial 0.30 | Reproducibility 0.53 | Confidence 0.40

**What is novel:** The individual theorems are well-known. What the system identifies is the *structural pattern*: three independent mathematical programmes (index theory, algebraic geometry, Morse theory) all assert the same principle — that singularities encode the whole. This convergence is the pattern itself, not the theorems.

**What is novel:** Cohomology is well-known as a unifying tool. ## 12. Limitations

### 12.1 Scope

Only 5 of 14 mathematics fields in Gnosis AI's taxonomy were analysed. The remaining 9 fields — including probability theory, logic, differential equations, combinatorics, and others — may produce convergences that do not reduce to constraint. The four structural principles and the fixed point are findings about these five fields, not about mathematics as a whole.

### 12.2 Confidence Levels

All 30 convergences fall in the "preliminary" (0.40–0.55) or "speculative" (0.33–0.39) confidence categories. None reaches "supported" (≥0.60) or "verified" (≥0.80). The EA validation is conservative — especially the strength formula, which penalises convergences supported by few results or by structural analogies rather than formal convergences. Nonetheless, no convergence in this catalogue has been independently verified by domain experts.

### 12.3 Single Model

Both survey and detection use Claude. Systematic biases in Claude's understanding of mathematics would propagate through the entire catalogue. The adversarial scanning mitigates but does not eliminate this risk.

### 12.4 Depth Consistency

The depth_consistency EA dimension defaults to 1.0 in v1. A proper depth consistency check — verifying that individual convergences are consistent with the meta-convergence findings they support — has not been implemented. This inflates all confidence scores by approximately 0.10 × (1.0 - true_depth_consistency).

### 12.5 Survey Completeness

Each domain was surveyed for 12–18 results. Major theorems may have been missed. The convergences found depend on which results the survey identified. A different survey might produce overlapping but not identical convergences.

### 12.6 Stochastic Variation

Temperature 0.3 means results vary between runs. We have not conducted reproducibility studies to measure run-to-run variation. The meta-convergence cascade and fixed point may be more stable than individual convergences, but this has not been verified.
